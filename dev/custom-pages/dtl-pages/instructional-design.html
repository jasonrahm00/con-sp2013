How We Do It

We use Instructional Design Theory and learning theory to guide our design and best practices. Instructional Design is a mixture of cognitive, developmental, educational, and social psychology. We apply our understanding of how learning happens at various developmental stages within educational settings while considering social relationships

Luckily, we have guiding theories to aid in design decisions.

Theories and Models (Instructional Design Central, n.d.1)

  • ADDIE: A model for a phased approach to developing courses and programs. The five phases are Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, and Evaluation.
  • Bloom’s Taxonomy: A theory by Benjamin Bloom which levels intellectual behavior of activities. Three categories are used to define behavior: affective, cognitive, and psychomotor. This taxonomy provides guidance for leveling of courses and programs.
  • Dick and Carey (1978): Also known as the Systems Approach Model, this model treats instruction as a system as opposed to each part in an isolated state. The system considers the interrelationship between context, content, learning, and instruction. This model emphasizes the elements all come together to achieve learning outcomes. Elements of note are the instructors and the learners.
  • Kemp: A nonlinear approach to instructional design by combining components of instructional design, development, and evaluation in a circular process. This allows for flexibility in the instructional design process and accounts for many factors, including student needs and characteristics, content and activities, resources and support services, and student assessment and evaluation tools and methods (Kemp Design Model, n.d.2).
  • Kirkpatrick: An process used for course and program evaluation done on four levels before, during, and after a program or course. The four levels are reaction, learning, behavior, and results.

Project Process

Our specialty is flexibility and coordination. In general, our project process follows this pattern:

  1. Initial Meeting
  2. Gathering Content, Resources, Information
  3. Follow-up Meetings
  4. Review Opportunities for Enhancements
  5. Write Report
  6. Determine Next Steps (i.e. Course Proposal to UCEC/GCEC)

Planning Details

In the beginning of a project, we’ll work together to create a clear picture of our opportunities courses and curricular ideas. From there, we’ll plan according to the answers to our questions, including developing a timeline and a meeting schedule.

Example Questions We Ask

  • How are you?
  • Is this a new course, course revision, or curricular change?
  • Where does this design project fit into the full curriculum or program?
  • What do you want the students to be able to do and know upon completion of your course/program?

When We Do It

The general timeline of our work takes about 3-12 months to complete. For larger changes with curriculum considerations (i.e. proposing changes to program outcomes) it will take longer than 12 months. Once a course or curricular change is approved, it may take 3-12 months to implement.
